Meeting Notes

11/07/2022

  • TQFTs to TQCs

Something I was happy to learn in this meeting was the definition of a TQFT. First we define a cobordism between manifolds $\Sigma_0$ and $\Sigma_1$ which is a compact manifold $M$ such that $\partial M = \Sigma_0 \sqcup \Sigma_1$. Then we have a category of cobordisms which we'll denote $\text{Cob}(d)$ which are $d$-dimensional orientable cobordisms. Note that the dimension of the cobordisms is $+1$ dimension of the cobordant manifolds.

  • The objects are $d$ dimensional, smooth, oriented, and closed manifolds.
  • Morphisms are cobordisms (so $d+1$ dimensional manifolds) $M$ with $\partial M: \Sigma_0 \to \Sigma_1$. $M$ is a morphism $M : \Sigma_0 \to \Sigma_1$.

Now if $\mathbb{K}$ is a field, a $(2+1)$ dimensional TQFT is an $\otimes$-Functor $Z: \text{Cob}(d) \to \text{Vec}(\mathbb{K})$.

This definition sets up some other nice things, like allowing us to call a TQFT Hermitian ($Z(-M) = Z(M)^*$ for $\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{C}$) and unitarity such that $Z(\Sigma)$ is a Hilbert space.

Somewhat unrelated we discussed anyons which are important because non-abelian anyons can be used in the contruction of a topological quantum computer (Kitaev, Freedman, Larsen, Wang). Abelian anyons seemed simple enough to define. For operators $T_1, T_2$ we have that $[T_1, T_2] = e^{i \theta}$. This means if we exchange the states of two (abelian) anyons then the state of the joint system only changes by some phase. I may write about this more either in future meetings notes or a modification of this one.
